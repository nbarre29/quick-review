## The Concept of Mortgage Hedging

Here is a breakdown of why lenders use a **hedge position** to manage this risk:

### The Risk: Interest Rate Volatility

When a lender agrees to lock an interest rate for a borrower (say, at **6.0%**), they are making a commitment for 30 to 60 days. During this lock period, interest rates in the bond market can fluctuate:

* **If Rates Go Up:** The market value of the borrower's **6.0%** loan immediately decreases because investors can now buy a newer loan at, say, **6.5%**. If the lender sells the 6.0% loan, they take a loss.
* **If Rates Go Down:** This creates **fall-out risk**. The borrower may decide to abandon the 6.0% loan and go to a competitor for a new loan at the lower rate (e.g., 5.5%). The lender has spent time and money processing the initial loan and has nothing to sell.

### The Solution: The Hedge Position

To offset these risks, the lender simultaneously takes a **hedge position** in the financial marketsâ€”usually by selling mortgage-backed securities (MBS) or Treasury bonds on the futures market.

1.  **If Rates Go Up (Loan Value Drops):** The loss the lender takes when selling the 6.0% loan is offset by the **profit** they make on their sold bond position.
2.  **If Rates Go Down (Borrower Walks):** The loss from the borrower walking away is offset by the **profit** the lender makes on their sold bond position.

By implementing this hedge, the lender ensures that the economic value of their loan portfolio remains stable regardless of market movements, allowing them to focus on originating loans without the paralyzing fear of interest rate risk.
